We present a theoretical framework for understanding the dynamics of reinforcement learning algorithms on the probability simplex. Using tools from differential geometry—particularly the Shahshahani metric—we derive the governing ODEs and SDEs that describe how probability distributions evolve during training. Our analysis reveals phase transitions between regimes of diversity collapse and preservation, governed by the quality of the reward signal.

Key Takeaways

1

Simplex Dynamics

Reinforcement learning induces natural gradient flows on the probability simplex. The Shahshahani metric reveals the intrinsic geometry where distances near the boundary diverge logarithmically.

2

Phase Transitions

A critical transition occurs based on judge quality \(J = 1 - \text{FP} - \text{FN}\). When \(J > 0\), correct convergence; when \(J < 0\), adversarial collapse.

3

KL Rescue

KL divergence regularization can "rescue" learning even under adversarial judge conditions, preventing complete collapse to incorrect modes.

4

Order Preservation

Under appropriate conditions, if one response starts more likely than another, it remains so throughout training—a key stability property.

Dynamics on the Probability Simplex

We study the evolution of probability distributions \(\mathbf{p} = (p_1, \ldots, p_K)\) on the \(K\)-simplex \(\Delta^{K-1} = \{\mathbf{p} : p_i \geq 0, \sum_i p_i = 1\}\). The gradient flow under the Shahshahani metric takes the form:

\[ \frac{dp_i}{dt} = p_i \left( A_i(\mathbf{p}) - \sum_j p_j A_j(\mathbf{p}) \right) \]

where \(A_i(\mathbf{p})\) represents the advantage function for action \(i\). This replicator-like dynamics naturally preserves the simplex structure while driving probability mass toward higher-reward actions.

Phase Diagram & Critical Transitions

The system exhibits rich phase behavior controlled by the judge quality parameter \(J = 1 - \text{FP} - \text{FN}\) and the KL regularization strength \(\beta\).

  • Correct Convergence (\(J > 0\)): The system reliably converges to the correct answer with exponential rate.
  • Frozen Dynamics (\(J = 0\)): At the critical point, the drift vanishes and dynamics become purely diffusive.
  • Adversarial Collapse (\(J < 0\)): Without regularization, the system converges to incorrect answers.
  • KL Rescue (\(J < 0, \beta > 0\)): Regularization stabilizes at non-degenerate fixed points.

Shahshahani Geometry

The natural geometry for probability distributions is given by the Shahshahani metric, also known as the Fisher-Rao metric:

\[ g_{ij}^{\text{Shah}}(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{\delta_{ij}}{p_i} \]

This metric has profound implications: small probabilities become "far away" in the geometric sense, requiring exponentially more gradient signal to change. This explains why RL dynamics naturally resist probability collapse.

Order Preservation

A fundamental question is whether the dynamics preserve the relative ordering of probabilities. We prove that under certain conditions, if \(p_i(0) > p_j(0)\), then \(p_i(t) > p_j(t)\) for all \(t > 0\). This order preservation property has important implications for the interpretability and stability of learned policies.
